Scenario

Tier III Hyperscale Data Centre

A Tier III hyperscale data centre hosting SaaS providers (e-commerce, design, accounting). Some services process payments via Stripe (PCI DSS L1). Typical data handled: customer profiles, usage/telemetry, configurations, and support data, treated as critical information assets.

Responsibilities

  • ISO/IEC 27001 governance and ISMS maintenance
  • Security of infrastructure and Stripe integrations (shared-responsibility clarified)
  • Third-party and supply-chain risk: assurance, SLAs, reviews
  • Assurance reporting to clients, auditors, and regulators

Data in Scope

  • Customer profiles and usage/telemetry
  • System configurations and support data
  • Payment processing data via Stripe (PCI DSS L1)
  • All treated as critical information assets
Narrative

This portfolio shows how an ISMS operates across policy, risk, controls, and supplier assurance in a Tier III, SaaS-focused data centre.

1
Policy sets scope, roles, objectives, and a 6-month review cadence, with change-triggered reviews. Evidence is tracked to owners and dates.
2
Risk Assessment and Treatment uses a 5x5 likelihood/impact model with inherent vs residual scoring and explicit risk appetite bands. Each risk is tied to an owner, treatment, target date, and evidence link; residuals are re-scored after control implementation.
3
Statement of Applicability maps a focused set of Annex A controls (Organisational / People / Physical / Technological) to the assessed risks with Applicability, Justification, Status, Control Owner, and Evidence location. A separate appendix lists all 93 controls to show breadth awareness.
4
Third-Party Risk Register extends assurance to suppliers (e.g. Stripe, ISP, MSSP, monitoring SaaS, facilities, UPS), including assurance evidence, review cycles, and a sub-processor micro-checklist. Where a provider acts as an independent controller (e.g. Tier-1 ISP), sub-processor fields are N/A.

Together, these artefacts provide an audit-ready view from risk identification to control selection and supply-chain assurance, with owners and evidence for every step.

Artefacts
Policy

Information Security Policy

Scope, roles, CIA principles, 6-month review cadence. ISO 27001 aligned.

+

Designed and built end-to-end as a solo portfolio exercise against a fictional Tier III SaaS data centre scenario, demonstrating practical ISO 27001 policy design.

1. Purpose

This policy defines the organisation's approach to protecting information assets within the Tier III hyperscale data centre environment supporting SaaS technology providers. It establishes principles for ensuring the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information in alignment with ISO/IEC 27001.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all:

  • Information systems, networks, and physical infrastructure operated within the data centre.
  • Employees, contractors, and third parties with access to these systems.
  • Information processed, stored, or transmitted on behalf of SaaS clients, including customer and business data.

3. Policy Statement

The organisation is committed to protecting information as a critical asset, meeting ISO/IEC 27001 and regulatory obligations, managing third-party risks including Stripe/PCI DSS dependencies, and embedding a culture of security awareness across the organisation.

4. Information Security Principles

  • Confidentiality: Access restricted to authorised users only.
  • Integrity: Information safeguarded from unauthorised modification.
  • Availability: Systems accessible to authorised users when required.
  • Accountability: Roles and responsibilities clearly defined.
  • Continuous Improvement: ISMS reviewed through audits, monitoring, and risk assessments.

5. Responsibilities

  • Executive Management: Ensure resources and leadership commitment to the ISMS.
  • Information Security Officer: Oversee policy implementation and ISO/IEC 27001 compliance.
  • Employees and Contractors: Follow security procedures, report incidents, protect information.
  • Third Parties: Comply with contractual security obligations and assurance requirements.

6. Review Cycle

This policy is reviewed every 6 months, and additionally following any significant change to the environment, client requirements, or regulatory landscape.

Risk

Risk Assessment & Treatment

5x5 matrix, inherent vs residual scoring, owners, actions, target dates.

+

Designed and built end-to-end as a solo portfolio exercise, demonstrating risk identification, scoring methodology, and treatment planning aligned to ISO 27001.

1. Purpose

This risk assessment identifies and evaluates information security risks relevant to a Tier III hyperscale data centre supporting SaaS providers. It considers risks from internal staff, external parties, regulators, and customers, aligned to ISO/IEC 27001.

2. Methodology

  • Risks evaluated using a 5x5 matrix with Likelihood and Impact scales.
  • Each risk assigned a numerical score (Likelihood x Impact) and categorised as Low, Medium, High, or Critical.
  • Risks mapped to relevant ISO/IEC 27001 Annex A controls.
  • Inherent and residual scores recorded, with residuals re-scored after control implementation.
  • Each risk tied to an owner, treatment action, target date, and evidence link.

Controls

Statement of Applicability

7 controls mapped to risks. Applicability, justification, status, owners, and evidence.

+

Designed and built end-to-end as a solo portfolio exercise, demonstrating targeted control selection, traceability from obligations to controls, and audit-ready documentation.

Sample Extract

This extract lists the ISO/IEC 27001 controls relevant to the identified risks in this SaaS data centre scenario. Each entry shows applicability, justification, implementation status, control owner, evidence location, and links back to the specific risks it treats. A complete reference list of all 93 Annex A controls (ISO/IEC 27001:2022) is included separately for awareness.

Why Include the Obligation List

The SoA includes an obligation type and name for each control, making it clear which external and internal requirements drive control selection. This captures legal and regulatory duties, contractual and customer requirements, certification and framework commitments, and internal policies. Including the obligation list provides traceability from requirements to controls, clarifies shared responsibility boundaries, supports audit readiness, and ensures that when obligations change the affected controls are reviewed.

Annex A Reference Rationale

This appendix lists the ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A control catalogue (93 controls) across four themes: Organisational (37), People (8), Physical (14), and Technological (34). Included to provide a complete reference baseline so reviewers can see the full control universe, validate scope decisions, and understand shared-responsibility boundaries. The listing is for awareness only and does not imply every control is applicable or implemented.

Supply Chain

Third-Party Risk Register

Suppliers and sub-processor checklist. Inherent vs residual scoring, review cadence.

+

Designed and built end-to-end as a solo portfolio exercise, demonstrating structured supplier assurance, sub-processor oversight, and defensible third-party risk management.

Rationale

This register exists to make supplier risk visible, owned, and testable in a SaaS data centre context. The data centre relies on external services for payments, monitoring, security operations, connectivity, facilities, and maintenance. Those dependencies introduce confidentiality, integrity, availability, and compliance risks that must be assessed, treated, and reviewed on a cadence.

What This Covers

  • Criticality and services provided, including data types processed and purpose
  • Jurisdiction and transfer basis, including any cross-border processing
  • Contract and SLA terms that affect security and availability
  • Assurance evidence aligned to expectations (ISO 27001, SOC 2, PCI where relevant)
  • Sub-processor oversight, change notifications, right to object, and flow-down clauses
  • Inherent and residual risk scoring with owners, treatments, and target dates
  • Review cycles, last review date, and audit trail of actions taken

Why It Matters

  • Clarifies shared responsibility boundaries across controller, processor, and sub-processor
  • Links supplier controls and assurances to risks in the register and controls in the SoA
  • Provides a defensible record for audits and customer due diligence
  • Reduces single points of failure and avoids silent drift in supplier posture over time

Where a provider acts as an independent controller and not a processor (for example a Tier-1 ISP handling traffic metadata under telecom and privacy law), sub-processor fields are recorded as N/A for clarity.
